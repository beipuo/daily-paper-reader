Symbolic regression is a powerful tool for knowledge discovery, enabling the extraction of inter-pretable mathematical expressions directly from data. However, conventional symbolic discovery typically follows an end-to-end, “one-step” process, which often generates lengthy and physically meaningless expressions when dealing with real physical systems, leading to poor model gener-alization. This limitation fundamentally stems from its deviation from the basic path of scientific discovery: physical laws do not exist in a single form but follow a hierarchical and progressive pattern from simplicity to complexity. Motivated by this principle, we propose Chain of Symbolic Regression (CoSR), a novel framework that models the discovery of physical laws as a chain of sym-bolic knowledge. This knowledge chain is formed by progressively combining multiple knowledge units with clear physical meanings along a specific logic, ultimately enabling the precise discovery of the underlying physical laws from data. CoSR fully recapitulates the progressive discovery path from Kepler’s third law to the law of universal gravitation in classical mechanics, and is applied to three types of problems: turbulent Rayleigh-B ´enard convection, viscous flows in a circular pipe, and laser-metal interaction, demonstrating its ability to improve classical scaling theories. Finally, CoSR showcases its capability to discover new knowledge in the complex engineering problem of aerodynamic coefficients scaling for different aircraft. 

# Introduction 

The essence of physical discovery lies in distilling universal laws from natural phenomena and then articulating them through concise mathematical formulations, thereby constructing a framework for understanding the physical world 1,2 . This endeavor is seldom a one-off event; rather, it is a cumulative, progressive process that begins with first principles, advances via the tireless work of successive generations, and gradually builds towards more systematic and precise theoretical edifices. For example, the rigorous geometric system derived axiomatically from Euclidean geometry; Newton’s law of universal gravitation synthesized from Kepler’s laws of planetary motion; and the extension of Euler’s equations for ideal fluids to the Navier-Stokes equations governing real fluids. This trajectory—from simplicity to complexity—epitomizes the intellectual triumph of scientific discovery. 1

While this progressive paradigm has achieved remarkable success throughout scientific history, it has relied more heavily on human intuition and deductive reasoning. As scientific problems grow in both dimensionality and nonlinear coupling, their complexity now approaches the boundary of what human cognition can directly process. Consequently, approaches based on intuition and deduction are confronting a bottleneck. To address this challenge, advances in data-driven paradigms, particularly machine learning, now offer powerful tools for uncovering hidden patterns in high-dimensional data 3,4 .Symbolic regression, as a class of data-driven methods characterized by its “white-box” nature, offers a distinct pathway for scientific knowledge discovery 2,5,6 . By systematically searching the space of mathematical expressions, this approach can directly identify differential equations 7,8 

or extract closed-form expressions from observational data 9–12 , thereby addressing previously intractable problems in modeling complex systems and advancing the automation of scientific discovery. However, conventional symbolic regression typically employs an end-to-end, one-step discovery strategy, which faces limitations when applied to complex physical systems characterized by high dimensionality, multivariate interactions, and multiscale features 13 . Owing to the com-binatorial explosion, symbolic regression struggles to discover physical laws involving intricate hierarchical couplings. This manifests primarily in two typical issues: on the one hand, pursuing high fidelity to data often generates excessively lengthy and uninterpretable expressions, which tend to overfit and thus exhibit poor extrapolation capability; on the other hand, pursuing parsimony and interpretability often comes at the cost of omitting certain physical information, resulting in models that lack accuracy and generalizability. These issues are difficult to reconcile within a one-step discovery process. The fundamental limitation of this one-step methodology stems from its conceptualization of scientific discovery as a static, one-off process, thereby overlooking the hierarchical and progressive pattern that scientific knowledge itself is constructed. The progressive discovery paradigm aligns more closely with the inherent logic of theoretical development, offering greater systematic coher-ence and higher value for engineering applications. For instance, when confronting the limitations of an existing model in terms of accuracy or applicability, a progressive strategy can be employed. This involves incrementally extending the original theory within different regimes, resulting in a new theory that subsumes the old while achieving enhanced generality and precision 14,15 . Alternatively, one can introduce asymptotic scaling corrections to the base scaling laws derived from classical methods like dimensional analysis, systematically collapsing multi-scale data from complex sys-tems onto a single, unified curve 16,17 . In machine learning, hierarchical architectures, exemplified by convolutional neural networks (CNNs), achieve progressive learning by extracting and fusing features layer by layer, advancing from local patterns to global laws 18 . Furthermore, incorporating physical constraints (such as conservation laws and symmetries) into the training process guides the model to maintain physical consistency as its structure and parameters expand 19 . This physical consistency, in turn, enhances reliable knowledge transfer and ensures theoretical compatibility within data-driven modeling frameworks. Then, can this progressive paradigm of scientific discovery be applied to data-driven knowledge discovery, enabling the discovery of complete theories in typical physics scenarios? Motivated by this concept, we integrate the strengths of both progressive and data-driven paradigms to propose Chain of Symbolic Regression (CoSR), a method for structured, interpretable physical knowledge discovery. This framework internalizes the primary modes of human knowledge discovery— invariance learning, multi-layered compression, and scaling transformation—into an assembly 2process of symbolic knowledge chains, forming a data-driven discovery framework for complex systems. Built upon symbolic regression as a white-box foundation, and drawing inspiration from the forward propagation and hierarchical representation principles of deep neural networks, CoSR models the discovery process of physical laws as a dynamically growing knowledge chain that evolves from simplicity to complexity: starting from raw data, it progressively extracts intermediate knowledge units with explicit physical meanings to construct hidden layers. The discoveries from each layer serve as augmented feature spaces input to the subsequent layer, ultimately composing complex physical laws and achieving the precise discovery of the knowledge structures embedded within data. This architecture strictly adheres to the progressive cognitive mode of “from simplicity to complexity” inherent in scientific discovery, characterized by clear physical prior constraints and hierarchical features. We demonstrate the potential of CoSR across multiple physics examples with complex hierarchical couplings, showing that CoSR can not only discover classical physical laws but also automatically uncover concise and interpretable new laws from data.

# Overview of Progressive Discovery 

We introduce Chain of Symbolic Regression (with its overall architecture and workflow illustrated in Figure 1), which systematically constructs physical knowledge chains through progressive steps. First, in the invariance learning stage, physical quantities are dimensionless on the basis of the Buckingham π theorem, achieving dimensionality reduction while maintaining dimensional con-sistency. Implicit symbolic regression is then applied to automatically uncover intrinsic constraint relationships within the data, laying the groundwork for subsequent modeling. Second, in the multi-layer compression stage, the dimensionless parameters obtained from the previous stage serve as inputs. A hierarchical symbolic regression approach is employed to progressively extract hierarchical structures among variables, constructing intermediate knowledge units with clear physi-cal meanings. Finally, in the scaling transformation stage, the established mathematical relations undergo necessary refinement and simplification via transformed symbolic regression (for example, ‘curves to lines’ to reduce complexity, and ‘multiple lines to one’ to unify scaling laws), yielding a final knowledge representation layer that achieves a trade-off among formal parsimony, physical meaningfulness, and predictive fidelity. The knowledge chain ultimately constructed by CoSR bears structural resemblance to symbolic neural networks 20 , yet they differ fundamentally: the knowledge chain based on CoSR is dynamically adapted rather than pre-specified; its construction process is bidirectionally driven—both bottom-up for progressive refinement and top-down for simplification and condensation. This framework enables us to precisely extract universal laws embedded within data while clearly revealing the transparent process of physical knowledge assembly, allowing observation and understanding of how complex laws emerge stepwise from simple physical building blocks. A detailed description of the methodology, including dimensional analysis, hierarchical discovery, implicit discovery and refinement discovery, is provided in the Methods section. To demonstrate the capability of the CoSR framework in scientific discovery, we conducted validation and application cases across multiple domains. This historical trajectory constitutes a complete physical knowledge discovery chain: data observation and induction (Kepler’s third law) → dynamical analysis (formula for circular centripetal force) → fundamental interaction (law of universal gravitation). In this benchmark, we employ the CoSR framework to recapitulate this scientific discovery process. # Discussion 

Existing approaches typically treat the target function F as a single expression to be discovered through direct search. This strategy not only triggers combinatorial explosion and overfitting but also 14 yields a multitude of candidate ‘truth formulae’ that defy interpretation—a phenomenon we term the ‘Rashomon gate dilemma’ of knowledge discovery 39 . In this paper, we introduce Chain of Symbolic Regression (CoSR), a framework grounded in the philosophy that complex physical laws are not monolithic expressions but rather structured systems assembled from multiple simple knowledge units with clear physical meanings through specific logical relationships. This epistemological shift has led us to abandon the traditional path of directly searching for the global function y = F (x)

in favour of a structured decomposition strategy that transforms high-dimensional search spaces into tractable hierarchical subtasks. Through three sequential tiers, CoSR systematically constructs symbolic knowledge chains: from dimensionally constrained dimensionality reduction via the Π

theorem and implicit function discovery, to hierarchical discovery driving multi-level knowledge unit extraction, and finally to transformation and reconstruction—together constituting a unified and self-consistent methodology for scientific discovery in complex systems. By decomposing symbolic regression into structured layers, this methodology reduces high-dimensional complexity while establishing a reproducible pathway for systematically uncovering unknown laws from data. This stepwise integration of information across layers enables the framework to move beyond surface-level correlations, excavating the inherent laws hidden behind variables and achieving structured representation of previously unresolved physical mechanisms. Rather than proposing a new symbolic regression algorithm, we harness the potential of existing methods through a progressive strategy that simulates the ‘assembly’ process of physical knowledge. This allows us to observe how complex laws emerge stepwise from simple physical building blocks—a perspective that ultimately serves the broader vision of AI for Science. At the application level, this paper systematically demonstrates the framework’s capability to address real-world scientific problems through five representative cases. In classical mechanics, the framework not only recapitulated the discovery pathway from Kepler’s third law to the law of universal gravitation but also extracted the reduced mass representation and automatically discovered the generalized Kepler’s law applicable to binary systems. In turbulent systems, the discovered linear scaling law not only satisfies the established 1/3 scaling exponent but also offers new theoretical perspectives on energy transfer mechanisms in turbulent thermal transport. In rough pipe flow studies, the constructed Widom-type scaling law successfully bridges the two classical regimes corresponding to the Strickler and Blasius laws, replacing previous piecewise scaling representations and achieving unified characterization of all three turbulent regimes in rough-wall pipe flow. In laser-metal interaction, the framework automatically identifies a material characteristic number X that enables natural physical classification of three materials, and discovers a modified keyhole number Ke ∗ that reduces prediction errors for aluminium alloys. In aerodynamic coefficient scaling problems, a parameterized non-power-product scaling function was established, whose parameters (c1, c 2, c 3) quantitatively reveal the essential distinctions between sharp and blunt bodies in terms of shock wave intensity, viscous effects and base drag, achieving unified characterization of aerodynamic mechanisms across different geometries. Although CoSR has demonstrated remarkable knowledge discovery capabilities across multiple physical systems, achieving the precise discovery of physical knowledge from existing data, certain limitations remain to be addressed. First, when processing high-dimensional complex systems, symbolic regression may generate multiple mathematically equivalent yet physically inconsistent expressions; currently, selection among candidates still relies on researchers’ physical intuition in some instances. Second, efficient coordination among the three discovery modes still depends on experience-based strategies. While the proposed progressive pathway of ‘invariance + hierarchy 15 + transformation’ has yielded promising results, future work requires developing more intelligent symbolic regression discovery strategies to achieve autonomous optimization of discovery pathways. Finally, while symbolic regression can discover new physical laws, it is constrained to uncovering relationships within predefined human-specified concepts (such as “force” in gravitation) rather than autonomously generating new concepts or paradigms. Future research should strive toward realizing a complete scientific discovery chain from concept creation to theoretical system construction. In summary, CoSR successfully transforms the scientific discovery philosophy of ‘from simplic-ity to complexity’ into a practicable methodology, offering new technical pathways and theoretical paradigms for automated scientific discovery. As research progresses, this framework holds promise for playing a significant role in broader scientific discovery tasks, ultimately advancing from assistive tool to AI scientist. 

# Methods 

# Dimensional Analysis 

Dimensional analysis is a methodological approach that uncovers physical laws by examining the fundamental dimensions of physical quantities—such as mass, length, and time. Its core principle lies in the requirement that mathematical expressions of physical laws must remain invariant under changes in the choice of measurement units 40,41 . In the context of knowledge discovery, dimensional analysis operates through three types of constraints. 

Buckingham π theorem : A relationship involving n physical variables and k fundamental dimen-sions, expressed as f (Q1, Q 2, . . . , Q n) = 0 , can be transformed into a relationship F (π1, π 2, . . . , π p) = 0 among p = n − k independent dimensionless π groups. This elevates specific physical problems to the level of universal scaling laws. 

Normalization : By selecting characteristic quantities such as a reference velocity or mass, physical variables are expressed as relative ratios—examples include the Mach number and reduced mass. This is a specific form of nondimensionalization. 

Dimensional Homogeneity : As the most fundamental constraint that any physical equation must satisfy, dimensional homogeneity requires that all terms on both sides of an equation, as well as those being added or subtracted, possess identical dimensional compositions. This ensures that the equation describes an intrinsic physical relationship rather than a numerical coincidence arising from a particular system of units. Data-driven dimensional analysis has revitalized this classical approach by integrating machine learning and large-scale data mining techniques. Researchers can now automatically identify physically meaningful dimensionless combinations from high-dimensional, complex observational or simulation data—and even uncover key scaling laws that are difficult to anticipate through traditional methods 16,27,42–44 .

Symbolic regression is a method for automatically discovering mathematical expressions from data, offering both accurate data fitting and interpretability through concise representations. # Symbolic Regression for Hierarchical Discovery 

Hierarchical discovery aims to actively search for composite functions of the form y = f1(f2(· · · fn(x))) ,where each subfunction fi corresponds to an interpretable low-dimensional manifold or physical mechanism 45,46 . This paradigm mirrors the multiscale philosophy in physical modeling, in which a complex macroscopic phenomenon y is decomposed into a chain of interactions among a series of intermediate physical quantities. The process is implemented using the evolutionary symbolic regression method PySR, whose multi-branch binary tree structure offers inherent flexibility for extracting two expressions in parallel (Figure 7a). To assess the validity of these intermediate 17 variables, we construct a loss function based on multivariate polynomial fitting: 

Loss H = ∥y − Fpoly (SR (x), β )∥22 (13) where x denotes the variables from the previous layer, SR (x) represents the intermediate variables extracted via symbolic regression, and the number of such variables typically decreases layer by layer (e.g., from three to two, eventually converging to one), forming an inverted pyramidal structure of compression. The target variable is y, and Fpoly (SR (x), β ) is generally a multivariate polynomial fit of order one to five. This loss function is designed to select, at each layer, intermediate variables that are both parsimonious and critically influential on the output (if an extracted expression is overly complex, it can be split into two or more components). The newly obtained intermediate variables are then used as inputs in an iterative process to discover subsequent layers, ultimately forming a hierarchical knowledge network composed of knowledge units with clear physical meanings. For further details on hierarchical discovery, see Supplementary Information 2.3.1. 

# Symbolic Regression for Implicit Discovery 

An implicit relation F (x, y ) = Const defines an intrinsic constraint that the system variables must obey, describing an isosurface or a constrained manifold in the state space of the variables, rather than an explicit causal mapping 47–50 . The core idea is that a robust implicit relation should exhibit a function value F (x, y ) that fluctuates minimally around a specific constant, while remaining sensitive to perturbations in any of the constituent variables. To achieve this, building upon the work of Zhong et al. 50 , we propose an improved loss function to guide the search process (Figure 7b): 

Loss I = Var[log( SR (x, y ))] + Psens + Pdim + Prule (14) Here, the variance minimization term Var[log( SR (x, y ))] enforces that all data points satisfy a constant constraint relationship. The sensitivity term Psens imposes a variable contribution constraint, expressed as: Psens = Pni=1 I∥SR (x, y ) − SR (˜ xi, y )∥22 < τ , where ˜xi denotes the input after applying random perturbations to the i-th feature, τ is the sensitivity threshold, and 

I[·] is an indicator function that returns a penalty value when the condition is met. This constraint ensures that each variable plays a substantive role in the expression, thereby preventing trivial solutions. The dimensional term Pdim enforces physical plausibility by requiring that the expression 

F (x, y ) adhere to the principle of dimensional homogeneity. The regularity term Prule imposes a constraint on the simplicity of the expression, prohibiting unreasonably large or small constants and nonsensical operations such as x − x or x/x . Implicit discovery need not be applied if no underlying implicit equation is not present. A detailed demonstration of implicit function discovery is provided in Supplementary Information 2.3.2.

Transformation discovery seeks to reformulate complex relationships into simpler forms within a transformed space through mathematical reconstruction 51–53 , thereby reducing the complexity of the expression (if no reduction in complexity is achieved, transformation discovery may be omitted).
